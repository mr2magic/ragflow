The Solar System: An Overview

The solar system consists of the Sun and everything gravitationally bound to it, including eight planets, dozens of moons, millions of asteroids, comets, and vast clouds of interplanetary dust. It formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago from the gravitational collapse of a giant interstellar molecular cloud.

The Sun contains 99.86% of the solar system's known mass. Its gravity holds the entire system together. The Sun is a G-type main-sequence star, burning hydrogen into helium through nuclear fusion at its core, where temperatures reach 15 million degrees Celsius.

The four inner rocky planets are Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars. Mercury is the smallest and closest to the Sun, with surface temperatures swinging from -180°C at night to 430°C during the day. Venus is the hottest planet despite being farther from the Sun, due to its thick carbon dioxide atmosphere creating a runaway greenhouse effect — surface temperatures average 465°C.

Earth is the only known planet to harbor life. It has liquid water on its surface, a protective magnetic field generated by its iron core, and an atmosphere rich in nitrogen and oxygen. Earth's single large moon stabilizes its axial tilt, moderating the climate over geological timescales.

Mars, the red planet, has the tallest volcano in the solar system — Olympus Mons, standing 22 km above the Martian surface. Evidence of ancient riverbeds and polar ice caps suggests Mars once had liquid water. Current missions are searching for signs of past microbial life.

Beyond Mars lies the asteroid belt, a region containing millions of rocky bodies left over from the solar system's formation. Ceres, the largest object in the asteroid belt, is classified as a dwarf planet and contains about one-third of the belt's total mass.

The four outer planets are gas and ice giants: Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. Jupiter is the largest planet, with a mass greater than all other planets combined. Its Great Red Spot is a storm larger than Earth that has persisted for at least 350 years. Jupiter has 95 known moons, including Europa, which has a subsurface ocean that may harbor life.

Saturn is famous for its spectacular ring system, composed primarily of ice and rock. The rings extend up to 282,000 km from Saturn but are only about 10 meters thick in many places. Saturn has 146 known moons; Titan is the only moon in the solar system with a dense atmosphere and liquid hydrocarbon lakes.

Uranus and Neptune are classified as ice giants, containing water, ammonia, and methane ices. Uranus rotates on its side with an axial tilt of 98 degrees, likely the result of a massive collision early in the solar system's history. Neptune has the strongest winds in the solar system, with gusts reaching 2,100 km/h.

Beyond Neptune lies the Kuiper Belt, a region of icy bodies including Pluto (now classified as a dwarf planet), Eris, Makemake, and Haumea. Even farther out is the Oort Cloud, a theoretical spherical shell of icy bodies that extends up to 100,000 AU from the Sun — nearly halfway to the nearest star. Long-period comets originate from the Oort Cloud.

The heliosphere, a bubble of solar wind created by the Sun, extends beyond the orbit of Neptune and shields the inner solar system from much of the interstellar radiation. The boundary where the solar wind meets interstellar space is called the heliopause; NASA's Voyager 1 spacecraft crossed this boundary in 2012, becoming the first human-made object to enter interstellar space.
