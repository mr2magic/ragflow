Galaxies: Islands of Stars in the Cosmos

Galaxies are vast collections of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter bound together by gravity. The observable universe contains an estimated two trillion galaxies, each hosting billions to trillions of stars. Galaxies come in a remarkable range of shapes, sizes, and ages, and their study has transformed our understanding of the cosmos.

The Milky Way, our home galaxy, is a barred spiral galaxy approximately 100,000 light-years in diameter. It contains between 100 and 400 billion stars and perhaps a similar number of planets. The Sun is located about 26,000 light-years from the galactic center, in a minor spiral arm called the Orion Arm. The Milky Way's central region harbors Sagittarius A*, a supermassive black hole with a mass of about 4 million solar masses.

Galaxies are classified by morphology into several main types. Spiral galaxies like the Milky Way and Andromeda have a central bulge surrounded by a flat disk with sweeping spiral arms rich in young blue stars and star-forming nebulae. Barred spirals have a straight bar of stars crossing the nucleus before the spiral arms begin. Elliptical galaxies range from nearly spherical to highly elongated, contain little gas or dust, and are dominated by older red and yellow stars with little ongoing star formation. Lenticular galaxies are intermediate, having a disk but no prominent spiral structure.

Irregular galaxies lack a defined shape and are often the result of tidal interactions or collisions. The Large and Small Magellanic Clouds, satellite galaxies of the Milky Way visible to the naked eye in the Southern Hemisphere, are examples of irregular galaxies. They are gradually being stripped of stars and gas by the Milky Way's gravity.

Galaxies are not evenly distributed through space. They cluster into groups and clusters held together by gravity. The Milky Way belongs to the Local Group, a collection of about 50 galaxies spanning roughly 10 million light-years. The Local Group's two largest members are the Milky Way and the Andromeda Galaxy (M31). These two spiral giants are on a collision course; in about 4.5 billion years they will begin to merge, eventually forming a giant elliptical galaxy sometimes called Milkomeda.

Galaxy clusters are among the largest gravitationally bound structures in the universe. The Virgo Cluster, the nearest large cluster, contains more than 1,300 member galaxies and lies about 53 million light-years away. Galaxy clusters sit at the nodes of the cosmic web — a vast network of filaments and voids that traces the large-scale structure of the universe.

Active galactic nuclei (AGN) are found in a fraction of galaxies and are powered by matter accreting onto supermassive black holes at their centers. Quasars are the most luminous AGN, outshining their host galaxies by factors of thousands and visible across the observable universe. The most powerful quasars release energy equivalent to a trillion suns. As the AGN phase ends, the black hole becomes quiescent, as likely happened with Sagittarius A*.

Dark matter plays a crucial role in galaxy formation and structure. Galaxies rotate in a way inconsistent with the mass of their visible matter alone; the outer regions rotate as fast as the inner regions, implying a large halo of invisible mass. This dark matter halo extends far beyond the visible galaxy and makes up roughly 85% of total matter in the universe. Its nature remains one of the deepest mysteries in physics.

Studying distant galaxies reveals the history of the universe. Because light takes time to travel, looking farther away means looking further back in time. The most distant galaxies observed by the James Webb Space Telescope existed within the first few hundred million years after the Big Bang, appearing small and irregular, gradually building up the rich diversity of galactic forms we see today.
