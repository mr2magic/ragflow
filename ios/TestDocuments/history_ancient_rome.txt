Ancient Rome: From Republic to Empire

Rome's rise from a small city-state on the banks of the Tiber River to the master of the Mediterranean world is one of history's most extraordinary stories. Over the course of a thousand years, Roman civilization shaped the laws, languages, and institutions of Western civilization in ways that persist to the present day.

According to Roman tradition, Rome was founded in 753 BCE by Romulus, its first king. Seven kings ruled before the Romans expelled the last, Tarquinius Superbus, in 509 BCE and established the Roman Republic. The Republic was governed by two annually elected consuls, a Senate of aristocratic advisors, and a complex system of popular assemblies. This constitutional balance was designed to prevent any single person from gaining too much power — a design that functioned, imperfectly but remarkably, for nearly five centuries.

The Republic expanded steadily through military conquest and diplomatic alliance. Rome's citizen-soldiers, organized into disciplined legions, proved superior to most opponents they faced. By the 3rd century BCE, Rome controlled most of the Italian peninsula. A far greater challenge came from Carthage, the powerful North African trading empire, in the three Punic Wars (264–146 BCE). The Carthaginian general Hannibal Barca crossed the Alps with his army and war elephants in 218 BCE, inflicting devastating defeats on Roman armies including the catastrophic Battle of Cannae (216 BCE), where an estimated 50,000–70,000 Romans were killed in a single afternoon. Yet Rome refused to surrender, rebuilt its armies, and eventually destroyed Carthage in 146 BCE, famously salting the earth where the city had stood.

The late Republic was wracked by social conflict and political violence. The Gracchi brothers, Tiberius and Gaius, attempted land reforms to address inequality between rich landowners and landless poor, and both were assassinated. Generals like Marius, Sulla, Pompey, and Julius Caesar used their armies as personal power bases, fighting civil wars for supremacy. Caesar's crossing of the Rubicon in 49 BCE — leading his army into Roman Italy, an act of treason — triggered the final civil war of the Republic.

Julius Caesar emerged victorious, was declared dictator perpetuo, and was assassinated on the Ides of March (March 15) in 44 BCE, by senators who feared he intended to make himself king. His death did not restore the Republic; instead it triggered another round of civil wars. Caesar's adopted heir Octavian ultimately defeated his rivals Mark Antony and Cleopatra at the Battle of Actium in 31 BCE. The Senate granted Octavian the honorary title "Augustus" — the revered one — and he became Rome's first emperor, though he carefully maintained the fiction that the Republic still existed.

The Pax Romana, roughly the first two centuries of imperial rule, was a period of relative peace and prosperity across a territory stretching from Scotland to Mesopotamia, from the Rhine to the Sahara. At its height, the Roman Empire governed perhaps 70 million people — roughly a quarter of the world's population. Roman engineering transformed the landscape: a network of 85,000 kilometers of roads, aqueducts carrying fresh water to cities, monumental public buildings including the Colosseum (completed 80 CE) and the Pantheon with its still-standing unreinforced concrete dome.

Roman law was perhaps Rome's most enduring contribution. The principle that all citizens were equal before the law, that the accused was innocent until proven guilty, and that laws should be written and publicly accessible — these ideas flowed from Roman legal thinking into the legal systems of Europe and their colonial descendants worldwide.

The western Roman Empire gradually weakened through the 3rd and 4th centuries CE under pressure from external invasions and internal instability. The empire split into eastern and western halves; the western half fell in 476 CE when the Germanic leader Odoacer deposed the last western emperor, Romulus Augustulus. The eastern half, the Byzantine Empire, continued for another thousand years until the fall of Constantinople in 1453 CE.
